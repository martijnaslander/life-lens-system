Weak Signals, Strong Patterns

What LinkedIn reactions reveal about your network — if you listen.

One post, eleven voices

On 13 April 2026, Monique Brinks published a LinkedIn post. She described how in 2003 she supervised a student scanning thousands of handwritten cards about resistance fighters from occupied Groningen. Then she described what happened twenty-three years later, when an AI system transcribed the same archive — 3,150 cards in 100 minutes — and cross-referenced it against five other archives to reconstruct an entire resistance network.

Within six hours, the post had 98 likes and 19 comments. For most people, that is where the information ends. Numbers on a screen. A notification badge.

The system saw something different.

The cross-reference

The system fetched the post, extracted the name of every commenter, and ran each name against a database of 21,330 known persons. Not a fuzzy match. Not a guess. A precise cross-reference: first name and last name together, verified against existing records.

Six out of eleven commenters were already in the database. That is not coincidence. It is a network responding to relevant content.

What the rings mean

The ring system is borrowed from how sensory neurons work in the brain. Not every stimulus reaches consciousness. The thalamus filters incoming signals based on strength and relevance. The rings do the same thing:

Ring 0 — your own signal. You reacted to the post yourself.
Ring 1 — core network. Strong existing connection, thick myelin. Immediate awareness.
Ring 2 — known. The person is in your database. The system recognises them.
Ring 3 — recurring unknown. Not in your database, but they've appeared before on other posts. This is sensitisation: a weak signal gaining strength through repetition.
Ring 4 — new. First time seen. Registered, but below the awareness threshold.

Ring 4 is noise until proven otherwise. Ring 2 is a known voice in the crowd. Ring 3 is the interesting one — someone you don't know yet, but who keeps showing up.

The signal you would have missed

Kees Verhoeven commented on the post. A former member of the Dutch parliament, writing about "serious attention" and a "responsive government." In a normal LinkedIn feed, that comment sits between nine others. You might see it, you might not.

The system didn't just see it. It recognised him. Kees Verhoeven was one of the first people who ever used the system, years ago. That history was in the database. The cross-reference surfaced it instantly. A weak signal in a feed became a strong signal with context.

Without the cross-reference, the comment is text. With it, it is a relationship that spans years, surfacing at exactly the right moment.

What happens over time

One post gives you a snapshot. Twenty posts give you a pattern. The system stores every reaction, every commenter, every ring classification. Over time, it answers questions that no single post can:

Who keeps coming back? Joost Geraets left two substantive comments on this post. If he comments on the next post too, he moves from ring 4 to ring 3. The signal is no longer noise — it is a pattern.

Which topics activate which networks? A post about AI and archives might attract archivists and politicians. A post about personal knowledge management might attract a completely different group. The overlaps — people who respond to both — are the bridges between your networks.

What is the sentiment trajectory? Is someone consistently supportive? Consistently critical? Asking questions that evolve over time? The system tracks sentiment per reaction, making it possible to see not just who responds, but how.

Looking outward

Most personal knowledge systems are introspective. They store what you write, what you read, what you think. They are excellent mirrors. But they are deaf. They don't hear what happens around you unless you manually type it in.

This is the first implementation of something different: a system that listens. It picks up public signals — reactions on a social platform — and translates them into personal relevance by checking them against everything the system already knows.

The LinkedIn feed showed 98 likes and 19 comments. The system showed six known voices, five new ones, one connection spanning years, and a mechanism to track what happens next.

Eleven reactions on a single post. Six known. Five new. The system didn't just read a LinkedIn post — it listened to a conversation and recognised the voices.
